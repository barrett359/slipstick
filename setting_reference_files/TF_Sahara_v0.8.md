# TF Sahara

**12/14/2432, 04:16 UST (Unified Solarian Time)**
**Solarian TF Sahara, Seraphim System**
**Vector: deceleration toward Veritas-083 "Gabriel"**

The eardrum-shattering tones of the emergency klaxon roused Vice Admiral Arnold Sanger from his rest with a knife straight to his temples. "MUNITIONS INBOUND. THIS IS NOT A DRILL," professed the overhead speakers, calmly and entirely too loudly. Momentarily confused, Sanger fumbled with his harness release with one hand while massaging the icepick in his brain with the other. Who would order a drill the morning after an arrival party?

"MUNITIONS INBOUND. THIS IS NOT A DRILL," thundered through his tiny quarters again, driving the icepick somehow deeper. At least the red lighting wasn't too bright.

His fingers found the clasp on his sleeping harness and he rocketed out of the bunk toward his medicine locker, his hangover the single most important thing in the universe for the moment. Every repetition of the klaxon sent a fresh wave of agony through his skull as he ripped open the wall locker, sending pill bottles spinning off across the compartment in the microgravity. There were more important things in there. "MUNITIONS INBOUND. THIS IS NOT—"

"Screw you, Xerxes," Sanger mumbled, and found what he was looking for, the syringe labeled acetylphenylpropionic acid. He locked the connector into the port on his left forearm just below the elbow and depressed the plunger. The relief wasn't instant, but it might as well have been.

The fog over his awareness sharpened as the sobriety meds did their work. Which of his captains was he going to have to dress down for this? You simply did not use the "this is not a drill" message, even if it did measurably improve crew performance. "Xerxes, mute the klaxons and report," he barked in the general direction of the ceiling speaker.

"MUNITIONS INBOU— Affirmative, Admiral. Emergency alerts locally muted," replied the crisp voice of Tiberius's ship ALI. The wall screen changed from a stunning scene of fiery rainbows dancing in Gabriel's upper atmosphere to a tactical plot. "At zero four hundred hours thirteen minutes, an unknown but significant number of hostile munitions eclipsed Gabriel's horizon on an intercept course for Task Force Sahara." The plot highlighted a cluster of red triangles with vectors, velocities, and intercept times as the ALI spoke.

Sanger stole glances at the plot while slipping into his rumpled shipsuit with practiced efficiency. "Time to intercept, fifteen to twenty minutes," Xerxes continued.

Quite the scenario. He'd have to commend whoever wrote this one, once they were finished scrubbing recycler filters. Sanger retrieved the combat stim syringe from half a meter above his magnetic boots, where it was still drifting slowly toward the deck, and injected it, banishing the last unwelcome remnants of Lieutenant Scolova's Titanian vodka.

"Xerxes, who ordered the drill?"

The ALI wasn't supposed to emote, but Sanger swore he could hear confusion in its voice. "Admiral, did you not hear the emergency message? This is no drill."

A minute and a half later, Sanger charged onto the CIC of BB-013 Tiberius. The bullets-on-battle-steel clanging of magboots rang through the compartment as primary officers scrambled for their stations ahead of and behind the admiral. Several junior officers scurried out of his way as if their lives depended on it while he barreled past the outer and inner rings of stations to leave an admiral-shaped hole in the hologram of the Veritas system they surrounded. He watched rainbows dance as the emitters replaced the no-longer-magnetically-contained water vapor, even as he undertook the five-second process of booting his command display and strapping in.

"Tactical, report," he shouted over the hammer blows of the last few stragglers running to their stations.

"Lieutenant Commander Vehnemea on Shinobi's Pride spotted the missiles three minutes thirty-seven seconds ago, sir." Lieutenant Scolova didn't look up from her console. "Still resolving contacts. At least ninety separate bogies so far. Acceleration is sporadic, averages about three and a half gees over the past hundred and eighty seconds, peaks past five when they dogleg. Tracking is damn hard with the maneuvering and the countermeasures. Three rounds of flares already, plus lidar dazzlers, radar jammers, chaff—"

"Standard burn-covering procedures, Lieutenant," Sanger interrupted. "Where did they come from?"

"Standby, sir." Scolova worked her console for a few seconds and then answered in the entirely too precise tone and cadence that meant she was trying to avoid the wrath of a superior. "Sir, we're not sure. The orbital mechanics say they have to come from Bogey Epsilon, since that's the only other fleet anywhere near Gabriel. But I can't confirm it. "The FUCK do you mean you don't have communications, Lieutenant?" Sanger shot a look at the comms station adjacent to tactical. "Lieutenant Spiner, confirm that."

"Confirmed, Admiral," the communications officer responded instantly. "In fact, sir, we have no communications with any satellite in the Veritas subsystem. Military or otherwise. It doesn't seem possible—"

"We can bitch about impossibilities later." Sanger grimaced. "Tactical, get me a delta-vee estimate on those missiles."

"Delta-vee estimate, aye," Scolova read back, as if she hadn't been working on exactly that since the second she'd booted her console. There was no time for pleasantries at the moment.

"Nav, report."

"Admiral, we were T-minus forty-nine minutes to circularization burn one when the missiles cleared Gabriel's horizon." As Lieutenant Colonel Ylsdóttir gave her report, the central hologram focused in on one of the hundreds of tiny dots orbiting the azure marble of the gas giant Veritas and its many moons. An extremely eccentric ellipse ringed the turbulent purple sphere of the moon Gabriel, a green arrow representing ==TF Sahara riding it inbound, one hundred fifty-two thousand kilometers out and still shedding six kilometers a second of approach velocity.== An orange tick mark for the first circularization burn sat farther along the curve. A second curve, just inside Sahara's own track, connected the task force to a cloud of red marks about eighty degrees around the moon's limb. Even as the admiral watched, the cloud crept closer.

"Admiral." Xerxes's voice was almost too perfect. "Admiral Ujichika has ordered formation Feline-Seven from Bonaparte. Transmitting vectors to helm now."

Toshiro was awake, then, and hadn't waited. Good. "Helm, engage Feline-Seven."

"Feline-Seven, aye, sir," affirmed Ensign Iapetus, even as she punched the throttle.

Tiberius shifted its metaphorical gears, feeding cold metallic hydrogen into a drive that had run pure fusion plasma for the entire voyage. As the propellant hit the plume, the vague suggestion of down that had marked the direction of thrust became a real pull toward the deck.

"Afterburner engaged, holding at point one two gees and climbing," reported the ensign at the engineering repeater station, whose name Sanger couldn't remember. I really need to do better than that, he thought.

Something else was bothering him. Something he couldn't quite place.

**04:30 UST**

There was no way for a human being to fight the next two minutes, so the humans did what they were actually for. They chose what to protect, and they chose what to believe, and the machines did the rest at ==kilohertz==.

The CIC's main hologram gave up on individual tracks and went to densities, a red weather front rolling in across a hundred-forty-thousand-kilometer map. Tiberius's main battery opened the engagement at ten thousand kilometers, ==ten module groups firing independently, each one a seventy-square-meter section of mirror putting a spot the width of two hands onto bodies the size of torsos.== At that range the light needed a fifteenth of a second for the round trip, and a torplet willing to thrust unpredictably at fifty gravities could be a full meter sideways of the aim point by the time the shot arrived. A meter was five spot-widths. Every pulse was a bet placed against a future the target hadn't chosen yet.

It was not shooting. It was statistics with a temperature.

The statistics said this: on a clean day, against this salvo, doctrine credited Tiberius's main battery alone with seven hundred kills before standoff, and the corvette spinals with hundreds more, and a salvo of four hundred should have died as a mild expenditure of lithium. ==Everyone on the CIC had run those tables at the academy.== Everyone on the CIC could also see the dazzle bloom on every receive channel, and knew what day this actually was.

"Group kills nominal," Scolova called, eyes on numbers no eye could follow. "Fifty, sixty a minute fleetwide and climbing as range closes—"

Then the numbers hit the wall the enemy had built for them.

Chaff first. The torplets were shedding it in gram puffs, aluminum nanoparticle sheets that bloomed into meter-wide clouds directly in the beam path. A seven-gram cloud died in half a second under a megawatt lidar, faster under the weapon itself, but the enemy had never intended the clouds as armor. They were punctuation. In the half second the beam spent chewing through, the torplet behind the cloud thrust sideways at a hundred gravities, the track filter's covariance bloomed, and by the time the lidar had clean sky again it was reacquiring, not tracking, and a fresh puff was already blossoming at the new position. On the raw optical feed the terminal volume strobed like a lightning storm, thousands of brilliant sub-second flashes of vaporizing aluminum, and under the strobe the warheads came on.

**04:41 UST**

The enemy did not press.

That was the first strange thing, and Sanger chewed it while the fleet bled and patched. Bogey Epsilon held its orbit, two thousand kilometers over Gabriel's cloud tops, tracking west across Sahara's sky at a stately clip. Its escorts exchanged occasional long-range fire with Tiberius, more conversation than combat at a hundred fifty thousand kilometers, ablative sparring that cost both sides paint and radiator margin. The CATHEDRAL fired in disciplined pulses and then went dark for long stretches, and Scolova's thermal watch put numbers to the pattern.

The duel between the big mirrors, while it lasted, was its own strange discipline. Both arrays were guns and eyes at once, which meant both crews faced the same indecent temptation: the most valuable square meters on the enemy ship were the ones looking back at you. Tiberius's groups walked their fire across the lasestar's face whenever the dazzle lifted enough for a solution, and the CATHEDRAL returned the courtesy, and both fire-control ecologies learned the same lesson at the same rate. A mirror is nearly invulnerable to light at its own design wavelength and nearly helpless everywhere else, and neither ship could hold a spot on one patch of a maneuvering target long enough at this range to matter much. What killed modules was not the beam finding glass. It was the beam finding everything between the glass: the actuator wells, the coolant runs, the phase metrology stalks in the segment gaps, the small hot machinery that let three thousand mirrors pretend to be one. A third of a second of dwell that would have opened her at dawn instead ablated a trench across her dorsal armor and boiled off two sensor masts, the wounded array's beam arriving wide and soft, its dead segments punching holes in its own focus. The alpha strike, paying its first dividend. Vagrant rolled fresh armor to the threat axis and kept station, and her captain sent a one-line status that got read aloud twice on the CIC: TRENCH, NO HOLES, STILL RUDE.

The enemy spent part of the hour on the drones, and Sanger let them. The flanking arc had gone active as receivers the moment the alpha strike ended, a hundred and one contacts of forty-megawatt nuisance, every one of them a bearing that had to be jammed separately, and Chandu's escorts went at the swarm with methodical light: a two-second dwell per drone at range, a small cost, repeated. What it bought them was arithmetic. Each drone was a mirror, a telescope, and a transmitter in a frame that cost less than the propellant a warship burned in a day; each kill spent escort heat budget and, more preciously, escort attention, whole minutes of fire-control time that did not get spent on the corvettes or the wave. The count fell to ninety, then to eighty-four, and somewhere around the twenty-third dead drone the killing simply stopped, the enemy's scheduler having finished the same arithmetic Sanger had started with. Seventy-eight drones still drifting, and the price of the other twenty-three formally logged as an hour of a battle fleet's undivided irritation.

At 06:34, in the middle of it, Spiner raised one hand from his console like a man in church.

"Receipt, sir." His voice cracked on the second word. "Rachel Station acknowledges. It's just the automated handshake, machine to machine, but it's pad-authenticated. They have the bundle. It's out, Admiral. No matter what, it's out."

Nobody stopped working. But the CIC's noise changed key, and stayed changed.

The enemy spent the hour repositioning with an economy that told Sanger more about Chandu than any intelligence brief ever had. One of the two surviving heavies eased forward of the CATHEDRAL with four light escorts, building a screening lattice between Sahara's wave and the lasestar: jamming pickets, chaff throwers, their own point defense layered in depth. Every move was correct. Every move was also fifteen minutes late, because every move was an answer to Sahara's missile launch, and the missile launch had been an answer to nothing at all. For the first time since 04:13, the machine on the other side of the sky was reacting.

At 07:05 it stopped believing.

The incoming SICKLE salvo, sixty-one buses arcing down the long flank toward a fleet-shaped lie, received its scheduled midcourse update, compared the picture against what its own seekers were beginning to resolve at closing range, and discovered the disagreement. Sanger watched it happen on the plot as a physical event: sixty-one drive plumes flaring at once, hard, lateral, the whole lattice wrenching itself onto new vectors like a flock startled off a wire.

"There it is," Scolova said. "They just found out we lied." She read the burn integrals off her filters with open satisfaction. "That correction cost them nine, maybe ten kilometers a second of terminal budget, and the lattice geometry is scrambled. They're re-solving the whole approach with four minutes to separation."

"It'll still be bad," Marchetti said quietly, from the ship's command circuit.

"It was always going to be bad," Sanger said. "Now it's bad and late. Stations for terminal defense. And Toshiro—"

Ujichika's voice came back with the compression stutter and something underneath it that thirty-one years let Sanger hear. "I know, Arnold. We're wearing your face. Try to look flattered."

**07:12 UST**

The second salvo came in ragged, mis-briefed, and nine kilometers a second poorer than planned, and it was still sixty-one fusion torpedoes, and Task Force Sahara met it with a fraction of the defense it had owned at dawn.

No Luna's Dream, and no spinal EWAR array quietly cutting lies out of the sensor picture. Two-thirds of the interceptor magazine gone. Nine corvettes where fifteen had been, their cell racks empty of everything but their last interceptors. What Sahara had instead was geometry: seventy-eight drones spread across seven thousand kilometers of flanking arc, awake now, every one a bearing the enemy's jamming had to solve separately, and none of them where the stolen picture said anything should be.

The interceptors flew their merge inside the enemy's own jamming umbrella this time, seekers boring straight down the CATHEDRAL's dazzle axis, and their kill rate showed it: a hundred eighty launched, nineteen buses killed. Tiberius's main battery, servicing targets between dazzle strobes, took seven more at ranges the manual called optimistic. Thirty-five buses reached separation, and two hundred eighty torplets came down the last twenty thousand kilometers into the fleet's fire.

It was ninety seconds of the same lightning-storm arithmetic as the morning, chaff strobing, kill rates climbing as ranges closed, the drones' parallax stitching truth together faster than the enemy could unpick it. The defense did better than it had any right to. It killed two hundred twenty-five.

Fifty-five lances reached standoff, and the enemy's targeteers, correcting late off their own seekers, split them the only way the scrambled geometry allowed: at the two biggest things still burning.

Bonaparte, wearing a battleship's signature, ate thirty-one.

Sanger watched the drone carrier take it on the optical feed, because he owed her that. The lances went in along her length in a rolling seam of light, hangar, spine, drive block, and when the last one faded she was still there, holed through in a dozen places, tumbling in slow degrees, drives dark, fifty thousand tonnes of proof of how much killing a big hull could absorb. Her transmitters were gone. Her lifeboat beacons started popping up along her flank within the minute, eleven, then fourteen of them.

Ujichika's beacon was not among them. It never would be. The command deck had been under the fourth lance.

Tiberius took fourteen, and fourteen was enough. The battleship staggered through the impacts with the main array falling to forty-one percent, the starboard rails fused into modern art, and a lance venting the compartments one deck up from the CIC to space in a single overpressure event that Sanger experienced as a white noise, a fist of deck, and then the bridge crew shouting numbers through smoke while the pressure doors sang.

His left arm was wrong. He filed it. On the ship's circuit, someone was calling for Marchetti and getting the ship's third officer instead, and the third officer's voice told the story before the words did. The captain's chair on the main bridge had been eleven meters from the breach. The captain had not.

"Kestrel's gone, sir." Scolova's voice, hoarse, through the smoke and his own pulse. "Halfpenny's hit but fighting. Six corvettes effective." A breath. "Bonaparte took the weight of it. The swap held. They shot the picture, sir. Right to the end, they shot the picture."

Sanger looked at the plot with his arm cradled and the smell of scorched insulation in the back of his throat. Amber and red everywhere. Six corvettes, a hulk full of lifeboats, and a battleship at forty-one percent, and every soul left alive around this moon was aboard something that could no longer survive the morning's battle over again.

The CIC worked its own wounds while it worked the battle. Two of the outer-ring stations were dark and bagged. Iapetus flew with her left arm strapped to her chest and her teeth set, refusing relief in a flat voice, on the grounds that her relief was in sickbay. A corpsman splinted Sanger's forearm at his station between his sentences, and he signed the after-action authorization for Marchetti's third officer to assume command of Tiberius with his right hand, in ink, on paper, because the young man would want it on paper someday and because there was, Sanger reflected, a certain fresh institutional wisdom in distrusting the network with anything that mattered.

Neither could the enemy. That was the whole plan, such as it was. On the plot, thirty-eight minutes out, two hundred sixteen missiles were still climbing at one relentless gravity, and the CATHEDRAL had a decision coming that Sanger would not have wished on any commander in any fleet, and he intended to make it worse.

"Damage control, priorities to the main array and the sinks," he said. "Scolova, time on our wave."

"Terminal at 07:45, sir."

"Then let's talk about heat."

**07:22 UST**

The lasestar's arithmetic was simple, and Xerxes laid it out in the dispassionate way of a mind that would never have to make such a choice with its own hull.

"The CATHEDRAL has been in intermittent combat for sixty-seven minutes on internal heat sinks. Modeling from observed fire discipline, its lithium reserves stand between seventy and eighty-five percent saturated. Our missile wave arrives in twenty-three minutes. Defeating a two-hundred-sixteen-missile saturation attack will demand its remaining point-defense capacity at maximum duty, which its sinks cannot carry. It must radiate before terminal, or it must accept thermal shutdown during it." A beat. "Its low-temperature radiator arrays deploy from armored bays. Deployed, they are five hundred meters of gossamer with a combined area of a small town. Admiral, it has to hang its life outside the armor, in our line of fire, in the next quarter hour."

"And it knows we know."

"It is likely computing that we lack the sustained output to punish the deployment meaningfully. Our own sinks are at ninety-one percent. That computation is close, Admiral. It may even be correct."

Sanger keyed engineering. "What's actually left in the tanks down there?"

The answer came back from the ensign whose name he had failed to remember at 04:18, and which he had learned at a crosstie junction an hour ago, holding a light. "Sinks will take about ninety more seconds of main-battery average before thermal cutoffs start dropping module groups, Admiral. After that we're firing whatever the hot loop can carry, which is a trickle." Ensign Park, engineering repeater, twenty-three years old. "If it matters, sir, cutoffs are a recommendation. I can pin them open. We'll eat module attrition."

"It matters. Pin them."

At 07:24 the CATHEDRAL made its choice, and Scolova called it in a voice gone soft with something adjacent to pity. "Radiator deployment, sir. Full low-temp arrays, both flanks. Screening lattice is thickening in front of it, chaff and jamming, they're going to try to hide the fans behind fog for fourteen minutes."

"Main battery," Sanger said. "Burn the fog. Then burn the fans."

What followed was ugly, attritional, industrial work, the kind of fighting that never makes the recruiting feeds. Tiberius fired in miserly pulses through gaps her own fire tore in the chaff, walking beams across radiator panels that glowed at a fragile seven hundred kelvin and died of megawatts. The enemy escorts threw everything into the screen. The lasestar's own wounded face answered when it could, and Tiberius's forward armor got thinner, and Ensign Park's pinned cutoffs let the ship cook herself by degrees, module groups browning out one by one like lights in a storm. At 07:39 the fleet's last two secondaries joined in from ranges that made their contribution largely spiritual.

By 07:41 the CATHEDRAL had lost something over a third of its deployed radiator area, re-retracted the remainder, and gotten perhaps sixty percent of the cooling it had gambled for. Sanger's board showed Tiberius's main array at thirty-three percent, her sinks blinking saturation, her big mirror half-blind and fully spent.

Both giants were done. Both knew it. And two hundred sixteen missiles did not care about anyone's lithium at all.

**07:45 UST**

The wave crested the enemy screen at a combined closing speed of fifty-plus kilometers a second, burning to the last, tanks nearly dry and their final gees climbing as the mass ran out, one thousand two hundred ninety-six torplets separating into the terminal dance. Sahara's drones lit their designators from the flank, seventy-eight clean bearings the enemy's surviving jammers had to argue with one by one. The corvettes dazzled from a third axis. Every lie Chandu's machines tried to tell had to be told three ways at once, and there wasn't time, and there wasn't heat.

It was the morning's battle inverted, and the CIC watched it from the other side of the physics with the particular quiet of people seeing their own worst hour happen to somebody else. The enemy screen did everything Sahara had done at dawn, and did it well. Interceptors streamed off the escorts to thin the buses early. Chaff walls bloomed across the approach lanes, kilometers deep. The escorts' point defense strobed until their hulls flickered like signage. And behind it all the CATHEDRAL burned, wounded face flexing, servicing kill queues through a third of an array and every degree of heat margin it had bought with its shredded radiators.

Scolova narrated the arithmetic of it because someone had to, her voice going flatter as the numbers converged. "Their kill rate's holding around six hundred a minute. That is genuinely unbelievable gunnery, sir, in case anyone was wondering what we just spent four hours fighting. Wave has ninety seconds of terminal remaining. They need a rate near nine hundred." A pause. "They're not going to get nine hundred."

Twice the lasestar's whole battery went dark for a full second, mid-slaughter: thermal cutoffs tripping somewhere in its cooked interior, the ship's own protective reflexes strangling its guns at six hundred torplets a minute. Twice it came back. The third time it came back slower.

The wave's torplets did what Solarian torplets were built to do, which was to be too many. They jinked on the enemy's duty cycle, because Xerxes had read that rhythm all afternoon and briefed the seekers before launch. They shed chaff into the dazzle. They spent themselves on the screen with the patient economy of a mass-production doctrine written by people who had priced every warhead against every defender's heat budget and liked the exchange rate. In ninety seconds of terminal defense the enemy killed something close to a thousand incoming warheads.

Close to a thousand was not one thousand two hundred ninety-six.

The remainder arrived as the fleet's due, with interest. The light escorts died first, shredded in the outer lattice. One of the heavies broke in half at 07:50, her drive section tumbling away in a slow contrail of vented plasma. And at 07:51, ninety-some Casaba lances converged on the lasestar from three axes inside four seconds, and the CATHEDRAL, cathedral window and killing floor, eight hundred meters of the most sophisticated destruction two civilizations had ever pointed at each other, went the way of Luna's Dream: a light, brief and total, and then a constellation, expanding.

The CIC of Tiberius did not cheer either. They had spent the whole day learning what that light meant on the other end.

Scolova cleared her throat and put the count on the plot, because it was her job. "CATHEDRAL destroyed. One heavy, three light escorts destroyed. Remaining Bogey Epsilon: one heavy, four light, withdrawing east under thrust." She sat back from her console for the first time in nearly four hours, and looked at her own hands as though they belonged to someone else. "That's the sky, sir. The sky's ours."

"The sky was always theirs to leave," Sanger said. "Watch them go. Log everything."

The remnant of Bogey Epsilon did not close to finish it. For ten minutes the last heavy put long-range fire into Tiberius with the methodical lack of malice of a worker closing out a shift, ablating armor, slagging the crippled main array's face, holing the drive block until the battleship's gearing failed safe and her thrust went to zero. An execution of the ship, indifferent to the people. Then, at 08:26, the five surviving Chanduran hulls broke Gabriel orbit and burned out-system at a third of a gee, toward the bridge point, toward the wider timetable, and did not look back, and did not transmit one word.

Silence, Sanger thought, was the most honest thing they'd sent all day.

**08:57 UST**
**BB-013 Tiberius, adrift**

Tiberius was dying the slow way, which shipwrights politely call survivable.

Her reactor was scrammed, her drives were scrap, and she rode her ballistic arc past Gabriel on batteries and stubbornness, cabin pressure holding in about sixty percent of her volume. The work now was the work after: cutting people out of compartments, matching lifeboat beacons to names, ferrying the wounded across to the corvettes in anything that held air. Shinobi's Pride and Cheyenne were alongside Bonaparte's hulk, taking off survivors through the hangar wound. The count stood at five hundred eighty-three dead across the task force, one hundred seventy-six wounded, and it would move again before the day was done, and everyone knew which direction.

Sanger sat at his command station with his arm splinted and strapped across his chest, because sickbay was for people who could not give orders through pain, and dictated the after-action into the maser queue in plain, flat sentences. What they'd seen. What they'd lost. What had worked: the pads, the parallax, the orbit that couldn't lie. What hadn't: everything upstream of it. He recommended Ujichika for the Solar Cross in one sentence, because Toshiro would have hated a paragraph, and Okoye and Marchetti in the sentences after that. Somewhere in the middle of it he stopped and asked the question that had been circling him for an hour.

"Xerxes. This thing in the network. It'll need a name for the reports."

"Fleet convention assigns reporting names to my discretion pending confirmation, Admiral. I have provisionally designated the intrusion PARALLAX."

Sanger considered it. The error you could only see with two eyes. One sealed recorder against one rotten archive. "That'll do," he said. "Log it."

At 08:57 Spiner crossed the CIC without hurrying, which after this day was itself an event, and handed the admiral a flimsy. Actual printed paper, off the maser deck's hardcopy unit, because pad traffic was decrypted by hand and existed nowhere else.

RACHEL STATION TO SAHARA ACTUAL. PAD 7 AUTHENTICATED. BUNDLE RECEIVED COMPLETE. NETWORK QUARANTINE ORDERED FLEETWIDE 0641. YOUR TRAFFIC IS THE FIRST HARD EVIDENCE IN THE SYSTEM. IT IS ALREADY MOVING OUTBOUND. RELIEF FORCE UNDERWAY, BEST TRANSIT NINE DAYS. HOLD WHAT YOU HOLD. SIGNED VERGARA, VADM, COMDESRON SERAPHIM.

And below it, appended in the clear, in the loose shorthand of one flag officer to another with the whole world listening:

ARNIE. WE BELIEVED YOU BECAUSE NOBODY COULD FORGE THE MATH. WHATEVER IS LEFT UP THERE, IT WAS ENOUGH. — R.V.

Sanger read it twice. He folded the flimsy along a clean line and put it in the chest pocket of a shipsuit that smelled of smoke and eleven hours, and for a while he didn't say anything at all.

The orders that remained were small ones, which was its own kind of mercy. Vehnemea, senior surviving captain afloat, took charge of the screen and the boat work, corvettes shuttling between the wrecks with their holds full of stretchers and their racks empty of everything. The drones went out on picket, a thin cold shell of eyes around a fleet that had learned the hard way what eyes were worth. Xerxes kept the maser talking, and started, on its own initiative, transmitting the crew manifest and survivor roster in the clear on the old distress bands, uncoded, for anyone with an antenna: names, just names, the one kind of traffic no one had ever bothered to weaponize. Nine days to hold. Sanger had held worse orbits with less. Not many, and not lately, but some.

"Sir." Scolova had come up beside his station. She had a squeeze bulb in each hand, and the label on the bottle tucked under her arm was in Titanian Cyrillic. "The wardroom's holed," she said, by way of explanation. "This was in my locker. It seemed—" She stopped, and shrugged, and let the bottle finish the sentence.

"Lieutenant, that vodka nearly killed me once today already."

"Yes, sir. It's persistent."

He took the bulb. Around the CIC the watch went on in its quiet ruin: Ylsdóttir refining the drift solutions that would keep the wrecks and the lifeboats findable, Iapetus with her arm bound to her side working the thruster board one-handed, Ensign Park's voice on the engineering loop, hoarse and steady, walking a damage control team through a battery crosstie. Spiner's dish still swept its nine-minute cycle, telling the truth to anyone with a pad, over and over, patient as an orbit.

On the main screen, unmagnified, Gabriel hung against the black with Veritas rising huge and banded behind it. The moon's upper atmosphere was alive with light: long, slow streaks of it, blooming and fading in ionized colors across the purple twilight arc. The debris of two fleets, coming down for hours yet. Casaba casings and mirror segments, drone frames and drive blocks, some fraction of Luna's Dream and some fraction of the CATHEDRAL, all of it giving its velocity back as fire, indistinguishable now, rainbows in the airglow.

Scolova followed his eyes to the screen. "It's almost pretty," she said. "If you didn't know."

"That's how they got us the first time." Sanger raised the bulb a few centimeters, to the screen, to the streaks of light, to five hundred eighty-three names and thirteen ships and the first morning of the war, and drank. The vodka was exactly as bad as he remembered.

"Now we know," he said.
